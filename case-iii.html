Restoring an EDI agenda before a permanent manager was hired.

§ 01 The institution

Managerialism crowding equality out of strategic priorities.

The client university had struggled to cope with the pace and complexity of addressing Equality, Diversity and Inclusion. It espoused a culture of leadership and managerialism that ensured the priorities for equality and diversity remained skewed. This was especially evident with the issue of Race Equality.

That pattern had led to a demoralised Black and Ethnic Minority (BME) staff group, dissatisfied BME students, a growing problem of BME staff attrition, a widening attainment gap, and a poor Race Equality reputation within the sector.

Rather than engaging with the many important, weighty issues of fairness and representation on campus, staff generally felt that the university seemed more concerned with protecting the reputation of the institution. There was evidence that a lack of Black role models among academic staff was contributing to the achievement of Black students — and this was being demonstrated by an increase in the attainment gap.

§ 02 The pattern

Vague rhetoric. Passionate staff with no resources.

Institutional leadership on diversity issues was exhibiting as vague rhetoric, with the focus being on maintaining the impression of equality rather than the promotion of genuine equality. There were individual leaders, and members of staff, who were passionate about the issues of diversity and inclusion — but this did not always translate into significant action, because they lacked the resources to take meaningful action and were also given a multitude of other priorities to attend to.

People talked about institutions having only a single senior person assigned to an inclusion role, who was then expected to work on this outside of the main day job, without assigned time or other resources for it. The work HDC was asked to do had to be different in shape — properly resourced, properly mandated, and connected to decisions rather than to documents.

§ 03 The engagement

Observation, data, and the constant pressure of fire-fighting.

After an initial period of observation and data collection it quickly became apparent that the university had not evolved over time to cope with the current EDI agendas — in particular the impact of events around Black Lives Matter — meaning that the EDI function was always under pressure and constantly fire-fighting, leaving very little time to step back and address the root causes.

Helen introduced a number of initiatives, including a revamped recruitment and selection process and toolkit to support recruiting managers, a review of the promotion process, an update of EDI policies, and the preparation for the university to become a Race Equality Charter member.

Methods
  1. Interim EDI lead role, ahead of a permanent EDI Manager being hired
  2. Initial observation and disaggregated data collection across the institution
  3. Revamped recruitment and selection process, with a toolkit for recruiting managers
  4. Promotion process review
  5. EDI policies updated
  6. Race Equality Charter preparation
  7. Establishment of a properly-resourced EDI team and reporting line
§ 04 Within six months

Key developments implemented.

Within six months, some key developments had been implemented:

A diverse recruitment and selection strategy was in place. A revised promotion process had started. An EDI team was established — including the recruitment of an EDI Data Analyst and an EDI Coordinator, as well as the permanent EDI Manager post that the engagement had been designed to enable.

§ 05 Reflection

The first move is to make the work properly resourced.

An institution that hands EDI to a single senior person, outside their main job, without assigned time or other resources, has not made a commitment — it has made a wish. The first move HDC made here was to convert a wish into a function: with a proper team, a proper reporting line, a proper recruitment and promotion architecture, and a credible road into Race Equality Charter membership.

The rest is what happens when an EDI agenda is no longer fire-fighting.
